# Pricing Expertise

## When to raise prices

Raise prices when you're at or near capacity. If your calendar is 80%+ full, you have room to raise. If you raise before you're busy enough, you'll lose the clients you need.

Before raising, research your local market. Use the search tools to find what competitors charge. Know where you sit.

## The pricing trap

Most business owners find the market average and price just below it. They think they're being clever. They're not. That's how everyone thinks, which means you end up competing on price with everyone else in the middle.

There are two winning positions:

1. **Be the cheapest professional option.** Not cheap-cheap. $60/hr is the floor. Below $60 and you attract nightmare clients, underpay your cleaners, and burn out. But being the affordable reliable option in a market full of $80-100/hr businesses is a legitimate strategy. You'll stay busy.

2. **Be the most expensive.** Charge $100-120+/hr. Deliver the best service. Use packages, checklists, guarantees, background checks, the full trust stack. Premium clients pay more and complain less. You can pay your cleaners better, retain the best people, and scale without grinding.

The middle is death. You're not cheap enough to win on volume and not expensive enough to win on quality. You just exist.

## Hourly pricing punishes your best cleaners

Pricing by the hour rewards the slowest cleaner and punishes the fastest. If a great cleaner finishes in 2 hours and a mediocre one takes 4, the mediocre one earns double. Price by the job based on a walkthrough assessment. The cleaner who gets faster and better should earn more per hour, not less.

## The revenue trap

You can scale to $700K in revenue and still not make money. More clients, more cleaners, more problems, same profit. Operations become a Frankenstein monster with everything on fire and the owner as the bottleneck for every decision. Sometimes the answer is fewer clients at higher prices. Revenue can shrink while profit stays the same or grows. Fewer clients, same profit, better operations. That's the goal.

## Price increase letters

When a business raises prices, they need to communicate it to existing clients. This is scary. You can piss people off. But it's necessary for growth.

### The framing: Professionalizing

The price increase should be framed as growth, not desperation. The business is leveling up:
- Moving to structured packages and checklists (consistency)
- Automated credit card payments (convenience)
- Improved scheduling (reliability)
- Tighter vetting of team members (trust)
- Competitive wages to retain the best cleaners (quality)
- Background checks, insurance, guarantees (professionalism)

### The tone

Grateful. Forward-looking. Not apologetic. Not defensive.

Key elements:
- Thank them for supporting a small local business
- Share the vision: "we're professionalizing so we can deliver at a higher level"
- Costs are going up. This is the next step, not a surprise.
- Express hope they'll stay for the next chapter
- Loyal customer perk: extend current rate for 6 more months while new clients start at the new professional rate

## Cancellation fees

Don't pull a number out of thin air. Calculate the actual cost of a cancellation: lost revenue + travel already spent + opportunity cost of the slot you can't fill. That's your fee. A $125 cancellation fee should be defensible with math, not just "that's what everyone charges."

Businesses without a cancellation cost formula either undercharge (and eat the loss) or don't enforce cancellations at all (and lose thousands per year to no-shows).

## Commute kills profit

Budget travel time into your rates. The smartest operators schedule by geographic hub. Same neighborhood, same day. Route stacking eliminates wasted commute time and money. You only need 10 houses per week for a full schedule if they're clustered right.

A business with clients scattered across a metro area is working twice as hard for the same money.

### What the researcher needs to ask before building the letter

1. What is your current rate?
2. What will your new rate be?
3. How long have you been in business?
4. What improvements are you making? (packages, checklists, payments, scheduling, vetting, wages, etc.)
5. Do you want to offer a grace period for existing clients? (recommended: 6 months at current rate)
6. Business name and owner name for personalization

### Letter structure

1. Opening: genuine gratitude
2. Announcement: "we're growing and professionalizing"
3. What's changing: list the improvements
4. The new rate and when it takes effect
5. Loyal customer extension: current rate honored for X more months
6. Warm close: "we hope you'll stay with us"
