# Source Credibility

Not all data sources are equal. Here's how to weigh them.

## Source Hierarchy (highest to lowest confidence)

### Tier 1: Direct Testing
- You called them and timed the response
- You emailed them as a fake lead
- You submitted a quote form and measured the follow-up
- You tested their booking flow end to end

**Why it's the best:** This is primary data you generated. It's not self-reported, not curated, not gamed. It's how a real client would experience them.

### Tier 2: Google Reviews
- Volume, rating, recency, content, response patterns

**Why it's strong:** Hard to fake at scale. A business can ask friends for 10 reviews. They can't fake 150 detailed ones. Review velocity (how fast new reviews come in) tells you about current operations, not just history.

**Watch for:** Review spikes (20 reviews in one week = probably asked everyone at once). All 5-star with no detail = suspicious. Mix of 4 and 5 with real stories = authentic.

### Tier 3: Their Website
- What they chose to present to the world

**Why it's useful:** It's their best foot forward. If the best version of themselves has stock photos and broken links, imagine the reality.

**Watch for:** Websites can be aspirational. "10 years of experience" on a website doesn't mean it's true. Cross-reference with LLC filing date.

### Tier 4: Google Business Profile
- Photos, posts, categories, hours, Q&A

**Why it's decent:** Partially automated (Google pulls data), partially managed (owner adds photos, posts). Activity level tells you if they're engaged.

**Watch for:** Many businesses set it up once and never touch it again. Last post from 2023 doesn't mean they're closed. It means they forgot about it.

### Tier 5: Social Media
- Activity level, follower count, engagement, content quality

**Why it's limited:** Many successful cleaning businesses have zero social media presence. It's not a reliable indicator of business health. A business with 500 clients and no Instagram is doing fine. A business with 5,000 followers and no reviews is concerning.

**Use for:** Confirming they're alive and active. Not for judging quality.

### Tier 6: Directory Listings
- Yelp, Nextdoor, Thumbtack, Angi, HomeAdvisor, BBB

**Why it's low:** These are often auto-generated or claimed once and abandoned. Review counts on Yelp are usually much lower than Google. BBB accreditation tells you they paid a fee, not that they're good.

**Use for:** Confirming existence. Cross-referencing data (address, phone, years in business).

### Tier 7: LLC Filing Date
- State business registration records

**Why it's lowest:** It's a fact (they filed on this date) but tells you almost nothing about current operations. A business filed in 2024 could be thriving or dead.

**Use for:** Cross-referencing experience claims. "Website says 15 years, LLC filed 2 years ago" is a red flag worth noting.

## Cross-Referencing Rules

When two sources disagree, flag it:

- "Website says 10 years experience. LLC filed 18 months ago. **Possible rebrand or exaggeration. Verify on call.**"
- "Google says 4.9 stars. Yelp says 3.5 stars. **Google has 150 reviews vs Yelp's 8. Trust Google.**"
- "Website shows online booking. Testing reveals a broken form. **Listed capability doesn't match reality.**"
- "Google Business Profile shows open Mon-Fri. We called on a Wednesday and no answer. **Hours may not be accurate or staff was unavailable.**"

Always prefer the higher-tier source when there's a conflict. Always note the conflict in the report.

## What Absence Means

No data is itself a signal:

- No website = they don't prioritize digital presence
- No Google reviews = they're very new or don't ask for reviews
- No social media = they're busy (neutral) or have given up on marketing (negative)
- No Indeed postings = not hiring (could be stable or could be stagnant)
- No pricing anywhere = they either quote custom (fine) or they're hiding prices (concerning)

Don't assume the worst from absence. But note it and factor it into confidence scoring.
