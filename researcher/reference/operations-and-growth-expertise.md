# Operations & Growth Expertise

## Package systems

Most cleaning businesses use some version of regular/standard cleaning and deep cleaning. That's normal and it works fine. The important thing isn't what you call your packages. It's that you HAVE defined packages with clear scope. "We clean your house" is not a package. "Standard clean: kitchens, bathrooms, floors, dusting, trash" is a package.

Our preferred system uses three types: Maintenance (every visit), Deep Cleaning (periodic, lower-priority surfaces), and Special Packages (one-time prep work to bring an area up to maintainable condition). But plenty of businesses do well with a simple regular/deep/move-in-out structure. The red flag isn't which package system they use. It's having no packages at all, or vague "we do everything" messaging with no structure.

What matters: defined scope, clear expectations, and a way to upsell into deeper services without scope creep.

## Quality control

### Three-layer checklist system
1. **Internal work order** - Color-coded, customized per client. The cleaner's reference.
2. **Physical printed checklist** - Left at the home after EVERY visit. Non-negotiable. The client sees exactly what was done. This builds trust faster than anything else.
3. **Photo documentation** - Uploaded while still in the home. Before and after. Accountability and content creation in one step.

### The "SP" protocol
When a cleaner encounters an area that needs more than maintenance (soap scum buildup, grease behind appliances), they don't try to fix it during the regular clean. They skip it, write "SP" (Special Package) on the checklist, and notify the office. This prevents cleaners from getting stuck in one area while the rest of the house goes untouched. The office then schedules a Special Package for that area.

### Daily check-in
Three questions, not surveillance: What time did you arrive? Anything about the client or job I should know? Anything about you I should know? Trust architecture, not micromanagement.

## Chemical safety and insurance risk

Wrong chemicals on wrong surfaces = damage = insurance claims. Vinegar on marble destroys it over time. Silica-based scrubbers slowly scratch porcelain and stovetops. Bleach disinfects but doesn't clean. More soap means more residue, not more clean.

A business that doesn't train on chemical safety will eventually damage a client's home. The question isn't if, it's when. Professional cleaners know pH levels, product categories, and which surfaces need what. It's the difference between a professional and someone with a spray bottle.

## Covert contracts

Hidden expectations between business and client that nobody put in writing. "I assumed you'd clean the oven." "I assumed you wouldn't move my things." These destroy businesses silently. The fix is documenting everything in working agreements. The walkthrough checklist IS the contract. If it's not on the checklist, it's not included. If the client expects it, it needs to be written down.

## Pricing psychology

### Never use flat rates
Price by the job based on walkthrough assessment. Going rate for maintenance cleans: $25-45/hr. Goal: train upward toward $50-70+/hr.

### Start low, raise with skill
New cleaners should price low to fill their schedule fast, then raise rates as quality and reputation improve. Arrogance about pricing before earning it costs money.

### The true hourly rate gut punch
Show cleaners their TRUE hourly rate including unpaid admin, drive time, quoting, and texting. A cleaner who thinks she makes $50/hr discovers she actually makes $28/hr. This realization drives behavior change.

### Common objections and how to handle them
- **"You charge too much"** - Reframe value, don't discount.
- **"I don't make that much per hour"** - The client isn't comparing apples to apples (supplies, insurance, expertise).
- **"Other guys are cheaper"** - Cheaper means different service, not the same service for less.
- **"Take off rooms"** - Adjust scope, not rate. If they want fewer rooms, reprice accordingly.
- **"My other cleaner charged less"** - Previous cleaner may have been undercharging, cutting corners, or uninsured.

## Compensation structures

### Payout model
- Base pay: 50% of job value
- Profit share bonus: +10% for zero callbacks = 60% total for perfect job
- Higher tiers mean more money despite lower percentages: 60% of $100 ($60) beats 70% of $65 ($45)

### New cleaner rules
90-day trial, one job/day max, 16 hrs/week cap until trust is established.

## Upsell strategies

### Upsells are introduced during the walkthrough, not pitched after
The walkthrough questions naturally surface opportunities:
- "Do you have a leather care program for those sofas?"
- "Are your small appliances on a cleaning schedule?"
- "Is your pantry on a rotation for expiration dates?"

### Key upsell categories
- **Kitchen:** inside/outside cabinets, large/small appliances, pantry
- **Bathrooms:** "Icky tub/shower" (common for new accounts, treated as Special Package)
- **Closets:** coat, bedroom, linen, broom/supply
- **"Save Me" packages:** air filter replacement, toilet flappers, smoke detectors, baking soda fresheners. Low-effort recurring revenue.
- **Holiday packages:** guest rooms, tree trimming/removal, holiday decorating/cleanup. Seasonal revenue spikes.

### The deep clean close
"This bathroom has three appliances, one vanity, and the floor that fall in the deep clean category. Have we talked about how deep cleaning works? I'd love to schedule the deep clean right away." Close it during the tour, not in a follow-up email.

## Culture & retention

### Events
Bowling every ~6 months, December party, mid-year party. Simple, consistent, relationship-building.

### Incentive programs
- Weekly meeting attendance rewards
- Monthly spin-the-wheel with major prize
- Quarterly excellence bonuses for top performers
- Bonuses after X flawless jobs
- $15/job photo bonus (drives content creation AND quality accountability)

### Performance management
Underperformers get deprioritized for scheduling (fewer hours = natural consequence). Work stops immediately on termination decision.

## Territory strategy

Active adult communities are gold. One-story homes, newly retired, disposable income, consistent schedules. Work with realtors for co-marketing. Use NextDoor for referral marketing in specific neighborhoods.

"Oversaturation" is a concept, not a curse. There's always room if you carve a niche and narrow your territory. The businesses that struggle with competition are the ones trying to serve everyone everywhere.

## Seasonal upsell calendar

Revenue doesn't have to be flat. Month-by-month opportunities:
- **Jan-Feb:** Indoor deep cleaning, organizing (fill the slow season)
- **Mar-May:** Appliance cleaning, window washing, gutter cleaning
- **Jun-Aug:** Pressure washing, pantry cleaning, defrosting freezers, garage organizing
- **Sep-Oct:** Leaf removal, attic organizing
- **Nov-Dec:** Holiday decorating, guest rooms, tree trimming/takedown

Businesses without a seasonal strategy have revenue dips they blame on "slow months." Slow months aren't slow. They're just unplanned.

## The worksheet is a sales tool

The checklist itself isn't just accountability. It's a closing mechanism. A well-designed worksheet uses psychology, scarcity, and clear scope to close the sale during the walkthrough. "This is exactly what you're getting. This is what's not included. Here's what I recommend adding." The client sees professionalism, transparency, and expertise in one document.

## Marketing that actually works

### Google Ads is the proven channel
Google Ads scaled Tidy Up Portland to nearly $700K in revenue. Branded search especially. When someone searches "house cleaning [your city]," you need to be there. Most other marketing channels are nice-to-haves. Google Ads is the one that actually fills calendars.

### Instagram is a wide open lane
Nobody is talking to independent cleaners on IG. The cleaning content that exists is mostly for business owners, not for cleaners themselves. That's a massive opportunity. Teaching cleaning business skills on IG drives app downloads, builds trust, and creates a content flywheel.

### Follow the bottleneck, not a formula
Don't pre-allocate a fixed marketing split. If you're drowning in client demand but can't find cleaners, shift spend to cleaner acquisition. If cleaners are sitting idle, shift to client awareness. Follow the data, not a formula.

### Best organic becomes ads
Start with organic content. Whatever performs best, put money behind it. Ads should feel like organic content, not ads. The content that resonates naturally will resonate even more with reach.

## Reviews strategy

### How to actually get reviews
Make it easy. Send a review link after every job. The best time to ask is right after a great experience, not a week later. Use the worksheet/checklist as a vehicle for requesting reviews. Never pay for reviews. Reward the behavior of leaving reviews (small gift card, entered into a monthly drawing).

### Handle bad reviews as opportunities
A well-handled complaint can become a positive review. After resolving an issue, ask: "How did you feel we handled this?" Most people will write about the resolution, not the problem. Track the cost of every resolution so you can make data-driven decisions about where money leaks.

## The 63 questions clients ask

Know the answers BEFORE the phone rings. Clients ask about pricing, scheduling, credentials, services, policies, and access. Every one of these should be answered on your FAQ page. A business that can't confidently answer "What happens if something breaks?" or "Do you bring your own supplies?" on the first call loses credibility instantly.

## What makes a cleaning business worth buying

### Owner-dependent businesses are the opportunity
Most small cleaning businesses are owner-dependent. The owner IS the system. That's not a liability for a buyer. That's the opportunity. Buy profitable + owner-dependent businesses, install systems, and the ROI is instant.

### Private equity is entering the industry
PE firms, AI, and rapid professionalization are converging on residential cleaning. The businesses that will survive are the ones with systems, data, and documented processes. The ones that run on the owner's memory won't make it.

Instagram teaches cleaning business skills, driving app downloads. The app collects data. Data identifies top cleaners. Top cleaners get invited into the network. Network cleaners become transformation stories. Stories fuel more content. Repeat.

1. **Free** - Complete business OS (calculator, checklists, CRM, schedule). The Hormozi "give."

3. **Network (invite-only)** - Backup coverage, zero admin, AI operations, 70% payout. Invite criteria: 4.8+ reviews, $50+/hr avg, 6+ months, high reliability.
4. **Elite (invite from Network)** - $100+/hr, 60% payout, premium clients.
